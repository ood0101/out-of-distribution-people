# Karan Singhal

Bridging health and AI

by

[Angela Haupt](https://time.com/author/angela-haupt/)

![](https://gcp-na-images.contentstack.com/v3/assets/bltea6093859af6183b/blt6f5b61c003ecb8e9/6998ce4425f3ee2bd764574e/T100-Health-2026-Singhal.png?branch=production&width=3840&quality=75&auto=webp&crop=3:2)

Courtesy of OpenAI

More than 40 million people turn to ChatGPT with health-care-related questions every day—a level of demand that helped spur OpenAI’s 2026 launch of ChatGPT Health, which lets users upload medical records and other personal information.Karan Singhal leads the Health AI team at OpenAI, a group of researchers dedicated to improving AI capabilities in health settings. He’s played a key role in the push into this sensitive space, aiming to prioritize the safety and reliability of the large language models that power ChatGPT Health. (OpenAI and TIME have a licensing and technology agreement that allows OpenAI to access TIME’s archives.) “The idea that you could have a companion in your pocket helping you navigate care has been a dream for a long time,” he says.

Singhal worked alongside more than 260 physicians from around the world to shape and refine the system, and partnered on a clinical [study](https://www.pendahealth.com/ "undefined") that tests how AI can function as a clinician copilot in care settings. That work has informed ChatGPT for Healthcare, a new suite of products designed to improve care and slash administrative burden. It’s already been deployed in facilities like Boston Children’s Hospital and Cedars-Sinai Medical Center.

Doustdar now heads the Danish company that makes the popular diabetes drug Ozempic and weight-loss medication Wegovy, both of which contain the GLP-1 compound semaglutide. In January, Novo Nordisk launched the first GLP-1 pill for weight loss, marking a significant step in making the drugs more convenient and less expensive to take than the injectable forms previously on the market. # Jennifer Mensik Kennedy

Championing nurses

by

[Charlotte Hu](https://time.com/author/charlotte-hu/)

Associate Editor

![](https://gcp-na-images.contentstack.com/v3/assets/bltea6093859af6183b/blt307acf48b7d129c5/6998ce4683ec377cf1c7b69c/T100-Health-2026-Mensik-Kennedy.png?branch=production&width=3840&quality=75&auto=webp&crop=3:2)

Courtesy of Faithie & Anna Ekbundit—American Nurses Association

Nurses are the connective tissue underlying healthcare systems, and studies have shown that they can cover care gaps in [physician shortage](https://time.com/6199666/physician-shortage-challenges-solutions/ "undefined") areas. Jennifer Mensik Kennedy is president of the American Nurses Association (ANA), which represents 5 million nurses in the U.S. and has been advocating for policies that support and protect [nurses](https://time.com/5835150/florence-nightingale-legacy-nurses/ "undefined") in their jobs. Recently, the group gathered a coalition of over 90 organizations to support the [ICAN Act](https://joyce.house.gov/posts/joyce-colleagues-reintroduce-bipartisan-bicameral-bill-to-increase-access-to-nurses "undefined") that was reintroduced in 2025, which would remove federal barriers preventing Advanced Practice Registered Nurses from providing care without doctor supervision. “The research has been clear for decades that nurse practitioners and other advanced practice roles provide quality patient care equal to, if not better than, primary care physicians,” says Mensik Kennedy. ANA also pulled together a [collective petition to Congress](https://www.nursingworld.org/news/news-releases/2025/department-of-educations-loan-proposal-puts-nursing-workforce-and-patient-care-at-risk/ "undefined") to requalify nurses as professionals following the U.S. Department of Education's new proposed definition of professional degrees, so loan limits don’t become a barrier to entry. Their activism has drawn international support. “We've gotten letters from across the globe,” Mensik Kennedy says. “The role of the APRN started in the United States. As countries are trying to develop the role, they look to the U.S. to help provide those standards.”
