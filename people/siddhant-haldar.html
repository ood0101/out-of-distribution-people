The spike is the research record on data-efficient robot manipulation. The Spike — With Evidence

The award line: four first-author papers, four data-efficiency wins

Haldar’s differentiator is not breadth of citations — it is a tight, first-author, award-winning body of work all pointed at one problem: learning robot manipulation from tiny amounts of data. He is first author on the papers that matter:

  • ROT / “Watch and Match” (CoRL 2022, Oral, Best Paper Finalist, ~116 cites). Supercharges behavior cloning with a regularized optimal-transport reward that matches agent and expert trajectory distributions — fast, reward-free imitation. His own framing of the program’s start.
  • FISH / “Teach a Robot to FISH” (RSS 2023, Best Student Paper, ~117 cites). Versatile imitation from ~1 minute of demonstrations: a learned offline base policy plus an online residual, with OT-based reward matching. Co-first with Jyo Pari. This is the result that established him.
  • BAKU (NeurIPS 2024, sole first author, ~117 cites). An efficient transformer architecture for multi-task policy learning that unifies observation and action modalities — the architecture work underneath data-efficient multi-task imitation. 130★ on GitHub.
  • Point Policy (CoRL 2025, sole first author, ~45 cites). Represents both observations and actions as semantic key points, enabling human-to-robot transfer with zero robot teleoperation data. This is the frontier of his program — eliminating robot-collected data entirely.

The throughline (his own dissertation framing)

His dissertation is titled “Data-Efficient Generalization for Robotics,” and he frames the research as “enabling data-efficient generalization for robot manipulation, spanning efficient real-world RL, architectures for offline learning, and observational priors for sample-efficient generalization.” The arc is unmistakable and accelerating: from algorithmic sample-efficiency (ROT, FISH) to efficient architectures (BAKU) to eliminating robot data entirely by learning from humans — key points, egocentric video, and touch (Point Policy, Point Bridge, EgoZero, Feel the Force, “Touch Begins Where Vision Ends”). That last vein — learn the skill from a human, transfer to the robot — is the single most-funded bet in robotics right now.
