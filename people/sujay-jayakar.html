◆ The Spike

Sujay Jayakar is a math major from Indiana who taught himself to code, interviewed at Dropbox in Haskell, and became one of the best systems engineers of his generation — all without a CS degree. He led the Dropbox Nucleus rewrite (Python to Rust, deployed to hundreds of millions of users), one of the most widely cited successful large-scale rewrites in the industry. Then he co-founded Convex ($53.5M from a16z), published at SOSP 2021 (top systems conference), and now leads infrastructure at Cursor — scaling to 192 shards, 1PB NVMe, 3M QPS at peak.

The pattern: someone who thinks from mathematical first principles, gravitates toward the hardest infrastructure problems at the most important companies, and repeatedly delivers at the highest level. Magic Pocket (exabyte-scale storage migration off S3), Nucleus (sync engine for hundreds of millions), kernel bypass networking (30 Gbps/core at Microsoft Research), Convex (reactive backend from scratch), Cursor (AI code editor at massive scale). Every role is the hardest problem at the company. Self-describes as “easily nerd sniped and okay with it.”

Background

Sujay Jayakar grew up in Munster, Indiana (Northwest Indiana). DECA international finalist (top 20) at Munster High School. Entered Cornell University (2009) and graduated with a BA in Mathematics and Economics in ~3 years (2012). No formal computer science training. Did undergraduate research with Robert S. Strichartz (renowned harmonic analysis mathematician) on lattice point problems in number theory, resulting in a published paper in Communications on Pure and Applied Analysis. Was originally going to work at the NSA doing cryptography, but the security clearance process dragged on.

His CS-major roommate brought him to a career fair where he met Dropbox recruiters. He interviewed in Haskell despite being a math major and was hired when Dropbox had ~100 employees (pre-IPO). Rose to Principal Engineer over ~8 years. Led Magic Pocket (migrating exabytes of data off S3 to Dropbox’s own storage infrastructure) and tech-led Nucleus — a complete rewrite of the Dropbox desktop sync engine from Python to Rust, deployed to hundreds of millions of users. The Nucleus rewrite is one of the most frequently cited examples of a successful large-scale rewrite in the industry. Also holds 5+ patents assigned to Dropbox covering sync, content item management, and namespace operations.

After Dropbox, spent time at Microsoft Research working on Demikernel — a kernel bypass TCP stack achieving 30 Gbps/core and 7μs RTT. Published at SOSP 2021 (the top systems conference). Then co-founded Convex (originally Zerowatt, Inc.) with James Cowling (MIT PhD under Barbara Liskov) and Jamie Turner. Raised $53.5M from a16z (Martin Casado led), with angels including Adam D’Angelo and Drew Houston. Departed Convex ~2025 to become Head of Engineering, Infrastructure at Cursor, where he now scales the AI code editor’s infrastructure (192 PlanetScale shards, 1PB NVMe storage, 3M QPS at peak).

The Overlooked Angle

The obvious read is “senior engineer at Cursor.” That misses the origin story and what it reveals. Sujay Jayakar is a math major from Indiana who has never taken a CS class. He interviewed at Dropbox in Haskell, got hired at ~100 employees, and over 8 years became a Principal Engineer who led one of the most famous rewrites in software history (Nucleus). He published number theory research with a renowned mathematician at Cornell and systems research at SOSP. He holds 5+ patents in distributed sync.

The through-line is first-principles thinking. Every system he’s built — Magic Pocket, Nucleus, Demikernel, Convex, Cursor infrastructure — required rethinking assumptions from scratch rather than applying conventional wisdom. The math background isn’t incidental; it’s the source of the engineering instinct. He approaches systems problems the way a mathematician approaches proofs: what are the axioms, what can we derive, and where does the current approach violate the invariants?

The NSA counterfactual is telling: he was going to do cryptography for the government, but the security clearance took too long, so he went to Dropbox instead. The entire trajectory — Principal Engineer, SOSP publication, Convex co-founder, Cursor Head of Eng — hinges on a bureaucratic delay at the NSA.
