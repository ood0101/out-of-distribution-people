Standard Intelligence (si.inc)

Self-described "aligned AGI lab." Small team in San Francisco. Three major shipped artifacts:

FDM-1 — First Fully General Computer Action Model

Trained on an 11-million-hour video dataset. Operates at 30 FPS. Can explore complex websites, complete multi-action CAD modeling sequences (e.g., gear extrusion in Blender), and drive a real car through San Francisco using arrow keys. Custom video encoder achieves 50× more token-efficient compression than prior approaches — encoding ~2 hours of 30 FPS video in 1 M tokens. Three-stage training pipeline: Inverse Dynamics Model on 40 K hours of labeled data → dataset labeling via IDM on full 11 M-hour corpus → Forward Dynamics Model training. Mouse movements tokenized via exponential binning into 49 discrete bins. ~50 % key press prediction accuracy on self-driving after minimal fine-tuning.

The Heap — 30 PB Data Cluster

30 petabytes of storage for under $500 K one-time cost, $17.5 K/month recurring. 38× cheaper than AWS, 10× cheaper than Cloudflare R2. Houses ~90 million hours of video for pretraining. Built in a 36-hour "Storage Stacking Saturday" (S3) — 2,400 enterprise hard drives (12–14 TB, SATA/SAS mix) into 100 NetApp DS4246 chassis. Software: 200 lines of Rust for writes, nginx for reads, SQLite for metadata. 100 Gbps dedicated internet via Zayo. 10 Intel RR2000 head nodes with dual Intel Gold processors.

hertz-dev — Open-Source Conversational Audio

8.5 B-parameter autoregressive model for interactive, full-duplex speech generation. First open-source base model for conversational audio. 80 ms theoretical latency, 120 ms real-world on a single RTX 4090. Architecture: hertz-codec (convolutional audio VAE, 16 kHz mono → 8 Hz latents, 1 kbps bitrate, 5 M encoder + 95 M decoder params, beats Soundstream and Encodec), hertz-VAE (1.8 B transformer decoder), hertz-transformer (6.6 B params, partially initialized from pre-trained LM weights, trained on 500 B tokens). Handles overlapping two-speaker audio natively. ~2× lower latency than any public model at release.

Personal Research (GitHub: calculating)

19 public repos. Every pinned project is a metalearning experiment in Jupyter Notebook:

  • architecture-distillation — Hotswapping a Llama attention layer with a Hyena convolution. Tests whether you can transfer learned representations between fundamentally different architectures.
  • meta-functions — Replication of the GPICL (General-Purpose In-Context Learner) paper. Core metalearning: training models to learn new tasks from context.
  • fast-weight-net — Experimenting with second-order gradient steps in PyTorch. Fast weights = the meta-learning primitive.
  • mnist-metalearner — In-context MNIST learning with gradient optimization on synthetic data. Learning to classify from a handful of examples.
  • batch-synth-img — Efficient batch generation for synthetic classification data. Infrastructure for metalearning experiments.
  • periodic-rnn — Testing RNN and FNN performance on periodic functions. Understanding inductive biases.

The Overlooked Angle

Look at the infrastructure choices. Most AI labs start with cloud credits and API keys. Galen's team built 30 petabytes of physical storage in a colocation facility in downtown San Francisco — 2,400 hard drives, 200 lines of Rust, a 36-hour stacking party they called "S3." They built a custom video encoder that's 50× more token-efficient than prior work. They open-sourced their audio model instead of gating it behind an API.

This is someone who builds from the metal up. Not cloud-native. Not API-dependent. Not VC-signaling. The 4× RTX 4090 personal rig he posted in January 2024 is the same instinct as The Heap — own the compute, own the data, own the stack. The Atlas Fellowship connection matters too: this is the EA/AI-safety-adjacent world, but he's building artifacts, not writing policy papers.
