◆ The Alpha — what’s actually special, measured against the field

Active inference has a thousand theory papers and almost no working robots. Pezzato is in the small set who actually closed that gap — and his distinct edge is a specific mechanism: use a massively-parallel physics simulator as the dynamics model and roll out thousands of futures in parallel (sampling-based MPC / MPPI), under an active-inference objective, so a robot does contact-rich manipulation reactively, online, with no policy training and no per-task reprogramming.

Why that’s the non-obvious move: the field forces a choice between learned policies (RL / imitation / the VLA wave — powerful but data-hungry and brittle when the world changes) and classical model-based control (MPC / task-and-motion planning — principled but slow to re-plan and bad at contact). Pezzato’s line dissolves the tradeoff: the simulator is the model (no model to hand-specify, no data to collect), parallel sampling makes it fast enough to be reactive, and it handles contact because the physics engine does. His RA-L 2025 paper on this is already at ~66 cites in well under a year — the rate-of-change tell — and it is, almost literally, auto·motion.

The second strand is Active Inference + Behavior Trees (T-RO 2023): reward-free, reactive task execution that recovers from failures and is interpretable — behavior trees giving structure, active inference giving the online adaptation. Same instinct as the company: model-based, data-efficient, reactive, the deliberate antithesis of the data-hungry end-to-end wave.

Calibrated honestly: 653 cites / h-10 is solid, not a citation superstar; the field-defining survey (2112.01871) is 3rd-of-11, he doesn’t lead it. The alpha is the first-author controller line (RA-L 2020 / T-RO 2023 / RA-L 2025), where he is one of the genuinely few credible to ship this — not the citation total.
