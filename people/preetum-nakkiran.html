The Spike — With Evidence

Deep Double Descent (ICLR 2020). Preetum is the sole first author, ahead of Gal Kaplun, Yamini Bansal, Tristan Yang, his PhD advisor Boaz Barak, and Ilya Sutskever. Submitted to arXiv Dec 4, 2019. The work was done during his OpenAI internship (with Sutskever) and posted on the OpenAI blog — this is the “OpenAI/Harvard era” lineage. The paper showed that, as model size grows, test performance first gets worse, then gets better — and that the same non-monotonic “double descent” appears as a function of training epochs. It is now a standard reference for the overparameterization story. [VERIFIED — arxiv.org/abs/1912.02292; Scholar: 1,575 cites]

Calibration theory. A sustained, technically deep line: A Unifying Theory of Distance from Calibration (STOC 2023, w/ Błasiok, Gopalan, Hu), When Does Optimizing a Proper Loss Yield Calibration? (NeurIPS 2023 Spotlight), and the 2025 Trained on Tokens, Calibrated on Concepts: Semantic Calibration in LLMs (arXiv 2511.04869, Preetum as first author). The throughline: how can models that are not Bayes-optimal still be “good” in a measurable way, and is there a meaningful, achievable notion of calibration for LLMs? The STOC paper shipped with open code (github.com/apple/ml-calibration). [VERIFIED — his site + arXiv]

The thesis (Harvard, July 2021): “Towards an Empirical Theory of Deep Learning.” Abstract, verbatim: “we take an empirical approach to the theory of deep learning. We treat deep learning systems as black boxes, with inputs we can control… and outputs we can observe… Our goal is to characterize how our choice of inputs affects the outputs.” Three pillars: (1) Deep Double Descent, (2) The Deep Bootstrap Framework (generalization reduces to optimization), (3) Distributional Generalization. This is a coherent, named research program — not a grab-bag. [VERIFIED — thesis.pdf, read directly]

Other load-bearing work: SGD on Neural Networks Learns Functions of Increasing Complexity (NeurIPS 2019, 376 cites — the SGD/generalization result), What Algorithms can Transformers Learn? (ICLR 2024, 256 cites, length-generalization), Normalizing Flows are Capable Generative Models / TarFlow (ICML 2025 Oral), and the widely-read Step-by-Step Diffusion: An Elementary Tutorial (Foundations & Trends 2024). [VERIFIED — Scholar]

In His Own Words (Primary Sources)

From his Gradient Podcast interview (“An Empirical Theory of Deep Learning,” ~97 min) — transcript pulled and mined directly. These reveal taste and the science-of-ML worldview.

  • “I want to study deep learning as an object of nature… like an object in the natural world… understanding deep learning means understanding the structure of this map [from design choices to the trained model].” — his core research framing
  • “Back then I really liked simple, clean, elegant ideas that I could understand… deep learning at the time was just way too messy for me… I didn’t even think it would be possible to understand it.” — the origin tension
