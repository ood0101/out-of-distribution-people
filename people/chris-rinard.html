The Spike

Chris Rinard’s path to Standard Kernel isn’t the clean narrative of “Stanford PhD starts GPU company.” It’s the story of someone who learned systems engineering at every layer of the stack — and learned what failure looks like — before arriving at the exact right problem. He built flight control systems for the ALIA-250 eVTOL at Beta Technologies, where a software bug doesn’t mean a 500 error, it means a crash. He TAed MIT’s legendary 6.172 Performance Engineering course, where he met Anne Ouyang. He raised $1M for a Web3 identity startup that didn’t work out — a failure that taught him the difference between interesting technology and essential infrastructure. Then at Stanford he found the essential problem: the missing compiler layer between AI algorithms and GPU hardware. Standard Kernel’s first artifacts — H100 BF16 matmul kernels in pure CUDA+PTX that beat PyTorch, and now Blackwell-optimized matrix multiplication — are the proof that this isn’t research theater. The flight controls background is the overlooked edge: he thinks about correctness and performance simultaneously, at a level where most ML engineers only think about performance.

The Overlooked Angle

Anne Ouyang gets the headline as the NVIDIA cuDNN engineer who identified the kernel bottleneck. Chris Rinard is the complementary co-founder who makes the company structurally stronger than a solo technical founder could. His background isn’t just “also good at systems” — it’s a different kind of systems thinking.

Flight controls for eVTOL aircraft operate under constraints that GPU kernel engineers never face: real-time deadlines, formal correctness requirements, and the knowledge that your code keeps people alive. That discipline — thinking about correctness and performance as inseparable, not tradeoffs — is exactly what you need when you’re building the compiler layer for AI hardware. A kernel that’s fast but wrong is worse than PyTorch.

The failed Web3 startup is the other overlooked signal. Most Stanford PhD students who start companies do it for the first time from the lab. Chris already has a failure behind him — he’s already learned what doesn’t work, how to raise money, and how to shut something down. Standard Kernel benefits from that scar tissue.

The MIT 6.172 connection is structurally important. This course is legendary in systems engineering — it’s where MIT produces people who think at the intersection of algorithms and hardware. Both co-founders TAed it. They didn’t just take the course — they taught it. That shared mental model is the foundation of the company.
