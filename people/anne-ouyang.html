The Spike

Anne Ouyang identified the bottleneck that everyone in AI infrastructure talks about but nobody was solving systematically: kernel engineering. She wrote CUDA kernels professionally at NVIDIA on the cuDNN team — the library that accelerates every deep learning framework. She studied it academically at Stanford under Azalia Mirhoseini (the researcher behind Google’s chip placement with RL) and Percy Liang (HELM, CRFM). She benchmarked it with KernelBench — a 250-problem suite that proved frontier models can’t even write correct CUDA kernels, let alone fast ones (o1 and DeepSeek-R1 match PyTorch baseline on less than 20% of tasks). Then she left her PhD to fix it by co-founding Standard Kernel, which already has H100 BF16 matmul kernels in pure CUDA+PTX that beat PyTorch benchmarks. The USACO Finalist and PicoCTF placements are the early signal: she thinks in systems, optimizes at the metal level, and has been doing it since high school. She’s building the compiler layer between AI algorithms and AI hardware — the most leveraged position in the entire stack.

Research & Publications

KernelBench: Can LLMs Write Efficient GPU Kernels?

“KernelBench: Can LLMs Write Efficient GPU Kernels?”
Scaling Intelligence Lab, Stanford · 2025

Open-source benchmark with 250 PyTorch ML workloads across 4 levels of difficulty: single operators (L1, 100 problems), fusion patterns (L2, 100 problems), full architectures (L3, 50 problems), and HuggingFace model architectures (L4). Introduced fast_p metric measuring correctness + speedup. Key finding: frontier models (o1, DeepSeek-R1) match PyTorch baseline on <20% of tasks. CUDA is a low-resource language in training data — models can’t learn to write it well from internet text alone.

KernelTreeSearch

Search-based methods for steadily improving LLM-generated kernels. Demonstrated progress on hard problems like flash attention generation. Part of the broader thesis: self-improving AI systems that get better at writing their own infrastructure code.

Agentic Reviewer

Building automated research paper review systems. Extending the self-improving systems thesis to the scientific process itself.

The Overlooked Angle

The obvious story is “Stanford PhD student starts GPU startup.” But the structural insight is deeper. Anne identified that the AI stack has a missing compiler layer. GPUs have gotten exponentially more powerful. Models have gotten exponentially larger. But the translation layer between algorithms and hardware — the kernels — is still written by hand, one architecture at a time, by a tiny number of specialists. She was one of those specialists at NVIDIA. She measured the gap with KernelBench. Now she’s closing it with Standard Kernel.

The competitive programming background is the early tell. USACO Platinum at 15, Finalist at 16. PicoCTF (cybersecurity). These are domains where you optimize within tight constraints — exactly what kernel engineering requires. The MIT Performance Engineering course where she met Chris Rinard is the bridge: it’s the course that teaches you to think about every cache line, every branch prediction, every instruction-level optimization. That course produced both co-founders of the company that’s trying to automate the hardest optimization problem in AI infrastructure.

The advisor pairing at Stanford is also telling: Mirhoseini (who used RL to design better chips) and Liang (who builds benchmarks to measure AI systems). Anne is the synthesis — she builds the benchmarks and the systems that pass them.
