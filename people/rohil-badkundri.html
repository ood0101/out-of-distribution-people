Evidence

ESM3: 98B-Parameter Protein Language Model (Science Cover, 2025)

Co-author on “Simulating 500 million years of evolution with a language model” — published on the cover of Science (Vol. 387, Feb 2025). ESM3 is a frontier multimodal generative model that simultaneously reasons over protein sequence, structure, and function.

  • Trained at three scales: 1.4B, 7B, and 98B parameters on 2.78 billion protein sequences and 771 billion unique tokens (1.07 × 1024 FLOPs)
  • Generated a novel fluorescent protein at 58% sequence identity from known GFPs — equivalent to simulating 500 million years of evolution
  • GitHub repo: 2.3k stars, 283 forks (evolutionaryscale/esm)
  • ~900+ citations on Semantic Scholar (as of early 2026)
  • Rohil’s specific contributions included conceptualization, data curation, formal analysis, investigation, methodology, software, validation, visualization, and writing

Dyno Therapeutics: Protein Language Models for Gene Therapy

Before EvolutionaryScale, Rohil worked at Dyno Therapeutics developing protein language models for AAV capsid engineering — designing gene therapy delivery vehicles using ML. This is where he first gained production experience applying foundation models to biological design.

Kreiman Lab (Harvard): Continual Learning Research

During undergrad at Harvard, worked in Gabriel Kreiman’s lab on continual learning and catastrophic forgetting. Published two papers: “Hypothesis-driven Stream Learning with Augmented Memory” (2021) and “Tuned Compositional Feature Replays for Efficient Stream Learning” (2021).

Early Work: CALM — Cholera Forecasting (High School, 2018–2019)

Built CALM (Cholera Artificial Learning Model) in high school at Lambert High School, GA — a system of 4 XGBoost models that forecast cholera cases across Yemeni governorates for intervals from 2 weeks to 2 months. Published on arXiv as a high schooler (arXiv:1902.06739). Also competed in iGEM 2018 (international synthetic biology competition) with Lambert team. Separately wrote code for cancer genomics research on the ubiquitin protein.

What He’d Build (Thesis Reconstruction)

Most likely: AI platform for clinical trial outcome prediction, differentiated by biological foundation model integration. Instead of predicting trial outcomes from clinical metadata alone (like QuantHealth) or molecular descriptors (like InClinico), Rohil could build a system that combines protein-level understanding (from ESM3-class models) with clinical trial data — predicting not just “will this trial succeed?” but “why will this drug-target interaction work or fail at the molecular level?” This is the “AlphaFold for clinical trials” thesis: bring structural biology intelligence into trial prediction.
