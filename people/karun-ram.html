The Spike

Karun Ram’s thesis didn’t just study concurrent algorithms — it machine-proved one correct. He wrote a three-part TLA+ formal verification that the Jayanti-Tarjan concurrent Union-Find algorithm is linearizable — meaning operations appear sequential even under concurrency. This is PhD-level distributed systems verification work, done as an undergraduate honors thesis. The algorithmic maturity required to even formulate such a proof sits at the frontier of theoretical computer science. He then paired it with near-perfect grades (3.99+ GPA, salutatorian of Dartmouth ’25), citations in three graduate-level CS theory courses, and a departmental teaching award for TA’ing the very courses that taught the foundations. The move to Optiver makes structural sense: someone who thinks in terms of provable correctness under concurrency is exactly who you want designing systems where microseconds and correctness both matter. The trajectory from Chennai to Dartmouth salutatorian to formal verification researcher to quant trading is a rare arc — pure theoretical depth deployed at production scale.

Research

Formal Verification of Concurrent Algorithms

“Machine Verification of Correctness for a Concurrent Union-Find Object”
Dartmouth Honors Thesis, 2025 · Advisor: Siddhartha Jayanti · Neukom Prize (2nd Place)

Proved linearizability of the Jayanti-Tarjan concurrent Union-Find algorithm via a three-part machine-certified verification in TLA+ (specification, invariant, proof). Union-Find is used to cluster large datasets and analyze large graphs/networks — e.g., finding connected groups on social media with billions of users. The concurrent version allows multiple processors to operate on the data structure simultaneously, but proving correctness under concurrency is notoriously difficult. This is typically PhD-level work in distributed systems verification.
