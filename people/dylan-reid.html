◆ The Spike

Dylan Reid’s career arc is unusually non-linear for a VC. While still a student at Cornell’s College of Architecture, Art & Planning studying computational design, he founded PopShop LLC (Ithaca’s first makerspace) and proposed Cornell Made — a university-wide initiative to accelerate Cornell-affiliated businesses, partnering the Kairos Society fellowship with Entrepreneurship@Cornell and Cornell Supply Management Services. He then became CEO of the Kairos Society, a global community of high-impact entrepreneurs operating across five continents. From there, he moved to Chile as Director of Business Development at Startup Chile / CORFO, helping transform the country into Latin America’s startup hub. He then co-founded Matter.io with serial entrepreneur Andy Palmer and Frank Moss (former director of the MIT Media Lab), building a digital manufacturing company that made 3D printing accessible to product designers. Matter.io was acquired by Junction. He was named to the Forbes 30 Under 30 in Manufacturing & Industry (2015) — the inaugural year that category existed.

Rather than starting another company, Reid pivoted into venture capital. At KEC Ventures, he built their in-house data platform from scratch and launched their entire pre-seed program, leading first-check investments in Fox Robotics (semi-autonomous forklifts), Mighty Buildings (closed-loop construction), Elementary Robotics (industrial inspection), and Bleximo (quantum computing). He then joined Zetta Venture Partners — the first fund exclusively focused on AI-driven B2B companies — where he has built an investment thesis around “latent sciences”: the idea that computation can collapse the latency of scientific discovery. His portfolio at Zetta is concentrated in AI-native biotech: Noetik (AI oncology, $14M seed from DCVC), Nabla Bio (advanced antibody design), Boltz Bio, Phylo Bio, Axiom Bio, Faro Health (AI clinical trials), Nanite (non-viral mRNA delivery), VideaHealth, Kaleidoscope Bio, and Mission Barns (cultivated meat). He sits on boards or serves as observer at several of these.

The out-of-distribution quality: Reid is not a PhD biologist or a career financier. He’s a computational designer who ran a global entrepreneur network, built a government innovation program in South America, manufactured physical objects with 3D printers, and then applied that exact same “latency reduction” lens to biology. His thesis — that self-supervised learning can bypass the labeled-data bottleneck in drug discovery, the same way digital manufacturing bypassed the tooling bottleneck in hardware — is a direct analogy from his Matter.io experience. He was investing in AI-native bio companies before the 2023 generative AI wave made it consensus.

Background

Dylan Reid grew up on Long Island, New York, attending Friends Academy (2004–2008), an elite Quaker preparatory school in Locust Valley, NY (A+ Niche rating, 20% acceptance rate, $47,900 tuition). He enrolled at Cornell University’s College of Architecture, Art & Planning in 2008, studying Computational Design — a program that sits at the intersection of design, engineering, and computer science.

At Cornell, Reid was deeply entrepreneurial. He was a Kairos Society fellow and proposed Cornell Made, an initiative to accelerate the growth of Cornell-affiliated companies. The program partnered the Kairos Society fellowship with Entrepreneurship@Cornell and Cornell Supply Management Services to create a “Cornell Mark” designation for student, alumni, and faculty companies. He also co-founded PopShop LLC (2012), Ithaca’s first makerspace, where he oversaw environmental design and interior work.

After graduating in 2012, Reid became CEO of the Kairos Society, a global community of high-impact entrepreneurs across five continents. He then moved to Chile as Director of Business Development at CORFO / Startup Chile (April 2012 – June 2013), where he helped transform the country into a startup hub for Latin America. During this period he also wrote for HuffPost, publishing articles on youth entrepreneurship including “Faced With A Grim Job Market, Young Entrepreneurs Create Their Own Employment” (2011) and “As Facebook IPO Nears, Universities Take Aim at Student Startups” (2012).

Reid then co-founded Matter.io alongside serial entrepreneur Andy Palmer (Koa Labs), Frank Moss (former director of the MIT Media Lab), Greg Tao, and Tobe Nwanna. The founding team first got involved with 3D printing at MIT. Matter.io built web-based software to let people customize 3D objects without training, initially focusing on jewelry before pivoting to small-batch manufacturing of metal parts. The company was based in Brooklyn and was backed by Metamorphic Ventures and others. It was acquired by Junction3D. Reid was named to the Forbes 30 Under 30 in Manufacturing & Industry (2015) — notably, this was the inaugural year the manufacturing category was added to the list.

The Overlooked Angle

The surface narrative — “founder sells company, becomes VC” — misses what makes Reid unusual. Three things stand out:

1. The Kairos/Chile chapter is the real origin story. Before Matter.io, Reid ran a global entrepreneur network (Kairos Society CEO) and then embedded himself in a government innovation program in a foreign country (CORFO/Startup Chile). Most founders-turned-VCs go straight from exit to Sand Hill Road. Reid spent formative years building entrepreneurial ecosystems from scratch — first across five continents with Kairos, then building Latin America’s startup infrastructure. This gave him a fundamentally different lens on what makes companies work: not just product-market fit, but ecosystem construction.

2. The design-to-biology bridge is non-obvious. A Bachelor’s in Computational Design from Cornell AAP is not the typical pedigree for someone investing in AI oncology and mRNA delivery. But the through-line is “latency reduction”: Matter.io collapsed the time from design to physical object (digital manufacturing). His bio thesis collapses the time from hypothesis to therapeutic (computational biology). The mental model is the same — use software to remove bottlenecks in physical-world production — just applied to molecules instead of metal parts. This cross-domain pattern transfer is rare and valuable.

3. He was pre-consensus on AI-native bio. Reid was investing in Faro Health (AI clinical trials) in 2019, Nabla Bio (AI antibody design) in 2021, and Noetik (AI oncology) in 2022 — all before ChatGPT made “AI + X” the default VC thesis in late 2023. He also built KEC’s data platform himself, suggesting he’s more technical than the average partner-track VC. His Noetik commentary about self-supervised learning bypassing the labeled-data bottleneck in drug discovery shows genuine fluency in the technical substrate, not just market thesis.
