The Spike

Aidan Smith is the person who makes the improbable seem casual. He started working full-time as a web developer at Chess.com at sixteen. He joined Neuralink at twenty and simultaneously attended Georgia Tech — supercommuting between Atlanta and the Bay Area. At Neuralink he did ML research for brain-computer interfaces, led speech applications, built the website, maintained the Link App, and developed cognitive testing games for primate subjects. Then he left without finishing his degree to co-found a $180M AI research lab. The Colorado Trail (500 miles), the untrained marathon, hiking Half Dome and Cloud's Rest in crocs on the same day — these aren't random adventure stories, they're symptoms of the same trait. He treats the gap between "I want to do X" and "I am doing X" as non-existent. In the Index Ventures profile, the Spectors picked him because he "embodies the infectious energy and sincerity the team brings to everything they do." He's the activation energy of Flapping Airplanes.

Background

Born ~2003–2004. Grew up in Mountain View, California. Attended Mountain View High School. Started professional software work early: security intern at Swaypay (~2019, age ~15), then full stack engineer at Stria Labs (2020). Hired as web developer at Chess.com in August 2020 at age 16 — stayed two years building features for one of the world's largest chess platforms (~100M users).

Enrolled in Computer Science at Georgia Institute of Technology. In December 2022, joined Neuralink as a software engineer while still at Georgia Tech — supercommuting between Atlanta and Fremont. Over three years at Neuralink: ML research for brain-computer interfaces, led speech applications for human subjects, wrote neuralink.com, developed cognitive testing games for primates, maintained the Link App. Left without completing his degree.

Received the Thiel Fellowship (Class of 2025, $200K) with a project called Innerphases — described as "human-native communication devices." Co-founded Flapping Airplanes with Ben and Asher Spector shortly after.

Twin sister studies media and human rights at UC Berkeley. GitHub Campus Expert and Arctic Code Vault Contributor.

The Overlooked Angle

The Thiel Fellowship bio writes itself: young dropout, cool companies, raised money. But the real pattern is about agency density. Most people who work at Chess.com at 16 stay in web development. Most people who join Neuralink focus on one domain. Aidan did ML research, led speech applications, wrote the public website, built games for primates, maintained the patient-facing app, and commuted across the country to attend college simultaneously. This isn't a resume — it's what happens when someone treats all constraints as negotiable. He started professional work at fifteen (Swaypay security intern), not sixteen as the Chess.com story usually begins.

The brain-computer interface experience matters for Flapping Airplanes' thesis more than it appears. The company's founding metaphor is that the brain is an existence proof of better learning algorithms — and that current AI could be 100K–1M× more data-efficient. Aidan didn't just read about the brain — he spent three years building software to decode its signals. The Innerphases Thiel project ("human-native communication devices") suggests he's still thinking about the brain-computer boundary even outside the lab. The mycology hobby is the same instinct: memorizing mushroom species is pattern recognition applied to biological systems. He thinks about intelligence as a phenomenon that exists across substrates, not as a software engineering problem. That's why the Spectors wanted him — he brings the biological grounding to a lab full of mathematicians.
