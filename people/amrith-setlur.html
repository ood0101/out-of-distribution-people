◆ Investment Thesis

Amrith Setlur is arguably the single most prolific researcher in test-time compute scaling for LLMs — the paradigm that letting models “think longer” at inference is more effective than scaling parameters. In 18 months he has published 7+ papers at ICLR, ICML, and NeurIPS that collectively define this field: process reward models that are 8% more accurate and 5x more compute-efficient than outcome models (ICLR 2025), RL on synthetic data that improves math reasoning 8-fold (NeurIPS 2024), meta-RL formalization of test-time compute (ICML 2025), theoretical proof that verification-free approaches are suboptimal (ICML 2025), and e3 — the first method to achieve genuine extrapolation beyond training token budgets (EXAIT Best Paper). He then built QED-Nano: a 4B model that matches GPT-OSS-120B on Olympiad-level proofs, demonstrating 30x compute efficiency. He is a final-year PhD student at CMU (graduating ~2026), dual-advised by Virginia Smith and Sergey Levine, collaborating with Aviral Kumar at the exact lab (CMU AIRe) producing the most cited test-time compute work in the field. He holds both the JP Morgan AI PhD Fellowship and CMU Presidential Fellowship. The window is now — he is at the classic “faculty or startup?” inflection point, and his research on making small models reason like large ones is the foundation for a compute-efficient inference company.

Evidence

e3: Test-Time Compute Extrapolation (EXAIT Best Paper)

“e3: Learning to Explore Enables Extrapolation of Test-Time Compute for LLMs” (co-first author with Matthew Yang). The key insight: most existing reasoning models do not extrapolate — performance plateaus beyond the training token budget. e3 solves this by training LLMs to perform in-context exploration: chaining operations (generation, verification, refinement) and testing multiple hypotheses before committing to an answer.

  • Three core elements: (1) chaining skills with asymmetric competence levels, (2) “negative” gradients from incorrect traces to enhance exploration during RL, (3) curriculum design coupling task difficulty with training tokens
  • e3-1.7B achieves top performance on AIME ’25 and HMMT ’25 among sub-2B models
  • Demonstrates genuine extrapolation to 2x the training token budget
  • Improves both pass@1 and pass@k relative to baseline

Process Reward Models: 8% Better, 5x More Efficient (ICLR 2025)

“Rewarding Progress: Scaling Automated Process Verifiers for LLM Reasoning” — first author. Defines process advantage verifiers (PAVs) that measure progress rather than step correctness. Collaborators include researchers from Google DeepMind (Jacob Eisenstein, Rishabh Agarwal, Alekh Agarwal).

  • PAVs are >8% more accurate than outcome reward models (ORMs)
  • 1.5–5x more compute-efficient than ORMs for test-time search
  • First result showing 6x sample efficiency gain for online RL with PRMs vs. ORMs
  • Theoretical proof: even weaker provers improve base policy as long as they distinguish steps

RL on Synthetic Data: 8x Math Reasoning (NeurIPS 2024)

“RL on Incorrect Synthetic Data Scales the Efficiency of LLM Math Reasoning by Eight-Fold” — first author. Key finding: training on model-generated incorrect solutions via per-step DPO is far more valuable than training on correct synthetic data.

  • 8x improvement on GSM8K and MATH benchmarks via per-step credit assignment
  • Shows positive-only synthetic data amplifies spurious correlations — flat or inverse scaling
  • Self-generated data + subsequent finetuning doubles efficiency of same synthetic problems

QED-Nano: 4B Model Matching 120B on Proofs (2026)

Built QED-Nano, a 4B parameter model post-trained for Olympiad-level mathematical proof generation. Combines supervised fine-tuning (distilled from DeepSeek-Math-V2 685B), long-horizon RL with reasoning cache, and structured test-time compute scaffolding.

  • 54% on IMO-ProofBench and 54% on ProofBench — competitive with Gemini 3 Pro
  • Matches GPT-OSS-120B (30x larger) on Olympiad proof problems
  • Test-time scaffold allows reasoning up to 1.5M tokens per problem
  • Fully open-sourced: model, data, code on GitHub and Hugging Face

Three ICML 2025 Papers

  • “Optimizing Test-Time Compute via Meta Reinforcement Finetuning” (MRT) — formalizes test-time compute as meta-RL. Co-authored with Matthew Yang, Yuxiao Qu, and Hugging Face researchers
  • “Scaling Test-Time Compute Without Verification or RL is Suboptimal” — theoretical proof that verifier-based RL methods dominate verifier-free approaches (distillation, cloning). With Nived Rajaraman and Sergey Levine
  • “What Do Learning Dynamics Reveal About Generalization in LLM Mathematical Reasoning?” — with Katie Kang, Sergey Levine, and Aviral Kumar

Market & Competitive Landscape

Test-time compute / inference scaling is the defining paradigm shift of 2024–2026. Pre-training scaling laws are plateauing — high-quality training data may be exhausted by 2028. The industry is pivoting: spend less on training, more on inference-time reasoning. Inference demand will exceed training demand by 118x by 2026, reshaping GPU procurement toward inference-optimized hardware. A 7B model with 100x inference compute can match a 70B model with standard inference.
