The Spike — With Evidence

First-author cancer research at NIH, age 16–18. Ally (listed as Yu A. Nakamura) published “Near-Infrared Photoimmunotherapy through Bone” in Cancer Science (2019, Vol. 110, Issue 12, pp. 3689–3694). The study demonstrated that NIR-PIT cancer treatment could penetrate bone tissue at 4.52% transmittance, significantly reducing tumor viability at 64 and 128 J/cm² irradiation — a novel finding for treating bone metastases. This was conducted at the Molecular Imaging Program, Center for Cancer Research, National Cancer Institute, NIH Bethesda. Co-authors include Hisataka Kobayashi and Peter L. Choyke (senior NIH investigators).

She is also second author on “Fluorescence-Guided Surgery” (Frontiers in Oncology, Dec 2017), co-authored with Peter L. Choyke at NIH — currently at 10,200+ views and 34+ citations. She was ~17 at publication. Publishing peer-reviewed cancer research at a top government lab as a high schooler is genuinely exceptional — this is not a coursework project.

Product shipping velocity at DoNotPay. At DoNotPay (Jun 2020–Sep 2021), she led the AI-enabled email client that launched Oct 7, 2020 — covered by Forbes, Wired, VentureBeat, and Digital Trends. DoNotPay Email used ML to classify incoming emails across 150 consumer rights categories and automatically apply refunds, cancel subscriptions, and fight spam. The feature was core to DoNotPay’s growth to 75K+ subscribers. DoNotPay had raised $12M from Coatue, Andreessen Horowitz, and Founders Fund. She was ~20 years old, shipping to national press at a company backed by tier-1 investors.

Hackathon creativity signal. What She’s Building: Catapult

The Product

Catapult (withcatapult.com, Catapult Technologies, Inc.) — self-described as an “AI agent agency.” Her personal site states she is “helping medical device companies deploy AI to make their workflows easier.” The company appears to have evolved from an initial healthcare-specific focus (trycatapult.ai domain) to a broader AI agent positioning (withcatapult.com). 1–10 employees. Based in San Francisco.

This sits at the intersection of two massive tailwinds: (1) the FDA cleared 295 AI/ML medical devices in 2025 alone, with 1,451 cumulative authorized devices, and (2) medical device companies need to integrate AI but face regulatory complexity (510(k) submissions, QMSR/ISO 13485 compliance, PCCP for post-market AI updates). Median 510(k) clearance time is 142 days, and 96% of AI medical devices go through this pathway.
