The Spike — With Evidence

(1) Cognitive Behaviors that Enable Self-Improving Reasoners (CoLM 2025 Oral)

Cognitive Behaviors that Enable Self-Improving Reasoners, or, Four Habits of Highly Effective STaRs — Gandhi, Chakravarthy, Singh, Lile, Goodman. arXiv 2503.01307 (March 3, 2025). CoLM 2025 Oral. GitHub: kanishkg/cognitive-behaviors (225 stars).

The paper introduces a framework analyzing four key cognitive behaviors that both expert human problem solvers and successful language models employ: verification, backtracking, subgoal setting, and backward chaining. Headline empirical finding: under identical RL training on the Countdown game, Qwen-2.5-3B far exceeds Llama-3.2-3B — and the difference is not training, it is the base distribution. Qwen naturally exhibits the four behaviors; Llama initially lacks them.

The genuinely surprising result — and the load-bearing one for the post-training thesis — is the incorrect-reasoning priming experiment. Quoting the abstract: “the presence of reasoning behaviors, rather than correctness of answers, proves to be the critical factor — models primed with incorrect solutions containing proper reasoning patterns achieve comparable performance to those trained on correct solutions.” Then: continued pretraining on OpenWebMath filtered to amplify reasoning behaviors (not correctness) enables Llama to match Qwen’s self-improvement trajectory. This is a structural result about what makes a base model RL-amenable, which is the central empirical question for every post-training lab in 2025-2026.

Mainstream press traction. The result was covered by CNBC (March 27, 2025) and NBC News in the “build-your-own AI boom” story about cheap reasoning models — with Kanishk identified as “lead researcher of the Stanford project that used Countdown.” This is the only paper of his with that level of mainstream penetration. Title nod (“Four Habits of Highly Effective STaRs”) is a deliberate signal of lineage to Eric Zelikman’s STaR (also Goodman lab) — which is the line that runs from STaR → Quiet-STaR → o1-style reasoning → R1 → this paper.

(2) Stream of Search — CoLM 2024 Oral, the pre-R1 paper on RL+search in LMs

Stream of Search (SoS): Learning to Search in Language — Gandhi, Lee, Grand, Liu, Cheng, Sharma, Goodman. arXiv 2404.03683 (April 8, 2024). CoLM 2024 Oral. GitHub: kanishkg/stream-of-search (153 stars).

The paper proposes a unified language-string representation of search trajectories — the “stream of search” — and pretrains a transformer from scratch on heuristic-solver- generated trajectories on the Countdown game (combine input numbers with arithmetic to reach a target). Quoting the abstract verbatim: “SoS pretraining increases search accuracy by 25% over models trained to predict only the optimal search trajectory.” Finetuning with APA + STaR then solves 36% of previously unsolved problems — including problems unreachable by any heuristic solver.

Why it matters. This paper introduced the Countdown task that DeepSeek R1-Zero and TinyZero later used as their canonical demonstration domain. The paper’s central insight — train on dense search traces including mistakes, then RL-improve — is exactly the recipe DeepSeek shipped a year later in R1. The fact that Stanford’s reproduction of R1-Zero (TinyZero, Jan 2025) used Countdown is not coincidence: TinyZero was reproducing R1 on Kanishk’s task. Co-author Archit Sharma is Chelsea Finn’s student (Stanford); Gabriel Grand is MIT (Tenenbaum). This is a strong cross-lab paper for a Y2–Y3 PhD student.

(3) Endless Terminals (Jan 2026) — the agents-with-RL extension

Endless Terminals: Scaling RL Environments for Terminal Agents — Gandhi, Garg (MSR), Goodman, Papailiopoulos (MSR + UW-Madison). arXiv 2601.16443 (Jan 23, 2026 v1; v3 Feb 14, 2026). GitHub: kanishkg/endless-terminals (111 stars).

A fully autonomous pipeline that procedurally generates terminal-use tasks without human annotation: 4 stages (task description → containerized environment → completion test → solvability filter), yielding 3,255 tasks across file ops, log management, data processing, scripting, databases. Trains agents with vanilla PPO + binary episode rewards. Headline numbers: Llama-3.2-3B 4.0% → 18.2%, Qwen2.5-7B 10.7% → 53.3%, Qwen3-8B-openthinker 42.6% → 59.0%. This is Kanishk extending from math-toy Countdown into the agentic-RL space — the same bet Reflection AI is making at $130M+ raised, the same bet OpenAI is making with operator/agent training. Co-authored with Papailiopoulos (his Microsoft Research collaborator from undergrad / pre-PhD years), so the MSR pipeline is still active.

(4) BigToM / Understanding Social Reasoning (NeurIPS 2023 Spotlight)

Understanding Social Reasoning in Language Models with Language Models — Gandhi, Fränken, Gerstenberg, Goodman. NeurIPS 2023 Datasets & Benchmarks Spotlight. Introduces BigToM — 25 controls + 5,000 model-written evaluations — a procedurally generated theory-of-mind benchmark using LLMs to populate causal templates. This is the paper that established Kanishk in the “cognitive-science of LLMs / theory-of-mind” line that Allyson Ettinger and Tom McCoy work in. Co-authors are all CoCoLab + CIClab (Gerstenberg). Goodman is senior. Jan-Philipp Fränken (2nd author) is now at Google DeepMind (Goodman alum on the frontier-lab pipeline).

(5) Earlier foundations — Baby Intuitions Benchmark + Mutual Exclusivity

Baby Intuitions Benchmark (BIB): Discerning the goals, preferences, and actions of others — Gandhi, Stojnic, Lake, Dillon. NeurIPS 2021. Cognitive-science benchmark adapted from infant-development experiments to evaluate whether neural models exhibit infant-style psychological reasoning. Coverage from NYU News, The Daily Beast, WNYC/Gothamist, The Jerusalem Post, NSF Science Now, ScienceDaily, New Scientist (per his own website). Companion: Commonsense Psychology in Human Infants and Machines (Cognition 2023, w/ Lake + Dillon).

Mutual Exclusivity as a challenge for deep neural networks — Gandhi, Lake. NeurIPS 2020. His earliest NeurIPS paper, with Brenden Lake at NYU.

(6) The TinyZero connection — Stanford’s reproduction of R1-Zero

Per The Daily Californian and CNBC (Mar 2025), Kanishk is identified as the “lead researcher of the Stanford project that used Countdown.” The Berkeley TinyZero project (Jiayi Pan, advised by Alane Suhr + Hao Peng) reproduced DeepSeek R1-Zero on the Countdown task that Kanishk + Goodman introduced in Stream of Search. The Cognitive Behaviors paper is, structurally, the Stanford-side analysis of why some bases self-improve under R1-Zero-style RL on Countdown and others don’t. The TinyZero + Cognitive Behaviors pair is the closest thing the academic world has to a Stanford response to DeepSeek R1.
