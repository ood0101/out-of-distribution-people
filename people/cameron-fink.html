The Spike

Cameron Fink didn’t build another chatbot or coding assistant. At 19, he identified that the entire $80B+ market research industry is built on a broken assumption: that you need to find and ask real people what they think. Aaru’s thesis is that you can simulate them instead — create synthetic populations that behave like real demographic groups, then test ideas, messages, and products against those simulations in real time. The validation is extraordinary: their Lumen model predicted the New York Democratic primary within 371 votes. That’s not a toy demo — it’s a proof that LLM-generated synthetic audiences can capture the statistical texture of real human populations. Accenture, EY, and IPG are paying customers. Political campaigns are using it for message testing. The $50M+ Series A at a headline valuation approaching $1B — with ARR still below $10M — is a bet on the category, not the current revenue. Cameron is the CEO who sells this vision: not “better surveys” but “the end of asking and the beginning of simulating.”

Background

Currently ~20 years old. Grew up in the Lake Forest, Illinois area. Attended Lake Forest Country Day School (Class of ’19, likely middle school). Self-taught programmer. Attended RSI (Research Science Institute) — the prestigious MIT summer research program for high schoolers. Had roles at Kleiner Perkins and was a Z Fellow before Aaru. Also co-founded Elda and had roles at Chainbird and Boutique Bites LLC.

Enrolled at Dartmouth College, then dropped out to pursue Aaru full-time. Co-founded Aaru in March 2024 at age ~19 with John Cole Kessler (CTO, 15 at founding) and Ned Koh (President, Harvard dropout). The company emerged from the insight that LLMs, properly calibrated with demographic and behavioral data, can simulate how populations would respond to stimuli — replacing traditional focus groups, surveys, and polling.

As CEO, Cameron drives business narrative and go-to-market. Mentored by Dave Burwick (former CEO, Boston Beer Company) and Frank Luntz (the political pollster). Returned to his former school (LFCDS) in February 2024 to mentor students on design thinking and entrepreneurship. Based in New York.

The Overlooked Angle

The press story is “teenager builds billion-dollar AI company.” The structural story is about market creation. Cameron isn’t competing with existing survey tools or adding AI to polling — he’s arguing that the entire paradigm of asking humans what they think is obsolete when you can simulate what they’ll do. That’s a $80B+ market (global market research) being attacked at the conceptual level, not the feature level.

The 371-vote prediction on the NY primary is the most important data point in the company’s history and it’s often buried in the press coverage. Traditional polling firms with decades of experience and millions in budgets routinely miss elections by thousands of votes. An AI simulation built by teenagers matched reality within 371. If that result is reproducible and generalizable, the implications extend far beyond market research — into policy simulation, urban planning, product development, and any domain where you currently need to survey humans.

The ARR-to-valuation ratio (sub-$10M ARR at ~$1B) tells you what Redpoint is actually buying: not a business, but a platform bet. They believe synthetic audiences will become a fundamental layer of how organizations make decisions, and they’re betting that Aaru will own that layer. Cameron’s job is to prove them right before the money runs out — and the enterprise client roster (Accenture, EY, IPG) suggests the go-to-market is working.

The co-founder age gap is the final tell. Cameron (19) and John Cole (15) aren’t playing at being founders — they’re building a company at an age when most people haven’t formed the assumptions that constrain how adults think about what’s possible. They didn’t grow up thinking surveys are how you learn what people want. They grew up with LLMs. Of course they think you should simulate instead of ask.
