The Spike

Asher Spector's research doesn't just advance theory — it ships as software that scientists actually use. His knockoffs work (Annals of Statistics, with Janson) spawned knockpy. His BLiP framework (JASA, with Janson) — which increased discovery power by 30–120% on UK Biobank and Sloan Digital Sky Survey data — shipped as both pyblip and blipr. His masked likelihood ratio statistics (JRSSB revision, with Fithian) are also in knockpy. This is someone who publishes in the most prestigious statistics journals and builds the Python/R packages so others can deploy the work immediately. The North American debate championship is the second tell — he thinks in frameworks, argues from structure, and wins when the game is about clarity under pressure. He's the analytical engine to his brother Ben's generative energy, and that complementarity is what Flapping Airplanes is built on.

The Overlooked Angle

The obvious framing is "statistics PhD joins AI lab." But look at where his papers land: Annals of Statistics, JASA, JRSSB, Journal of Political Economy, Financial Analysts Journal. These aren't ML conferences — they're the journals that define how scientists, economists, and investors reason under uncertainty. His software ships to geneticists working with UK Biobank and astronomers analyzing the Sloan Digital Sky Survey.

The industry stints tell the same story. He didn't just intern at quant funds — he published in the Financial Analysts Journal with his Stanford advisor. He didn't just work at Cursor and Mercor — he was embedded with their research teams. This is someone who treats the boundary between theory and deployment as something to be actively dissolved.

That's the skill set for an AI research lab that's trying to break paradigms — you have to argue rigorously for positions that the field hasn't accepted yet.
