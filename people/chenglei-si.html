The Spike — With Evidence

1. “Can LLMs Generate Novel Research Ideas?” (ICLR 2025) — the Nature-covered paper

Lead author with Diyi Yang and Tatsunori Hashimoto (arXiv:2409.04109). The first large-scale, statistically powered human study of LLM research ideation. The design is the spike: he recruited 100+ expert NLP researchers, had them write research ideas under controlled conditions, generated a matched set of LLM ideas with an ideation agent, standardized style across both (so reviewers couldn’t tell which was which), and ran a blind review across novelty, excitement, feasibility, and effectiveness.

Headline finding: LLM-generated ideas were judged statistically significantly more novel (p < 0.05) than expert human ideas — but slightly weaker on feasibility. Of the top-10 ranked ideas in one cut, nine were from the AI, one from a human. The paper is the empirical bedrock the entire AI-Scientist field cites. 507 citations (Scholar). Covered by Nature News and Newsweek; he did a long-form interview on the Cognitive Revolution podcast as lead author.

2. The Ideation–Execution Gap (ICLR 2026) — the Science-covered follow-up

The intellectually honest sequel (arXiv:2506.20803). Having shown LLM ideas sound more novel, he asked the harder question: do they hold up when actually executed? He recruited 43 expert researchers to execute randomly-assigned ideas — either human- or LLM-generated — with each researcher spending 100+ hours implementing the idea and writing a 4-page paper. After execution, review scores for the LLM ideas dropped significantly more on every metric (novelty, excitement, effectiveness, overall) — erasing and in several metrics flipping the ideation-stage advantage. Human ideas ended up scoring higher. The finding: the perceived novelty of LLM ideas is partly a mirage that collapses on contact with execution. Got Science coverage. This is the more important paper for an investor: it is the empirical case for why current AI-Scientist products are bottlenecked at execution, not ideation.

3. Towards Execution-Grounded Automated AI Research (ICML 2026)

The constructive third act (arXiv:2601.14525), with Zitong Yang, Yejin Choi, Emmanuel Candès, Diyi Yang, Hashimoto. If ideation is cheap and execution is the gap, then automated research must be execution-grounded — evaluated on real experimental outcomes, not on how a proposal reads. This is the throughline of his program: ideation (2025) → the execution gap (2026) → grounding research in execution (2026). Plus Predicting Empirical AI Research Outcomes with Language Models (NeurIPS 2025, w/ Jiaxin Wen, He He, Shi Feng) — using LMs to forecast whether a research direction will work.

4. Design2Code (NAACL 2025) — the widely-cited benchmark

“How Far Are We From Automating Front-End Engineering?” (w/ Yanzhe Zhang, Zhengyuan Yang, Ruibo Liu, Diyi Yang). A benchmark for multimodal code generation: screenshot → working front-end code. 178 citations, a standard reference for vision-to-code / agentic-coding evaluation. Shows his range: not only science automation but the broader “can AI do the work of a professional” question.
