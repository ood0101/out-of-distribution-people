◆ The Spike

Cory Levy cold-emailed every VC he could find starting at age 15 in Houston. TechStars said yes first. Then Union Square Ventures. Then Founders Fund. At 19 he tweeted at Keith Rabois and got $1M from SV Angel, True Ventures, General Catalyst, Naval Ravikant, Gary Vaynerchuk, and Michael Dearing. He dropped out of UIUC, built After School to 20M users, got Forbes 30 Under 30 twice, and then — instead of raising a traditional fund — did something nobody else does: he grinds it out campus by campus, hackathon by hackathon, visiting 25+ universities a year, running fireside chats at midnight because “builders work late,” offering $10K bounties for the coolest project at HackIllinois, personally texting with thousands of founders. The result is Z Fellows: <1% acceptance rate, 500+ alumni in Slack, multiple cohorts per year, and a portfolio that has produced 3 unicorns (Whop, Aaru, Owner.com), 5+ companies at $100M+ valuations (add Hebbia at $700M and Novig at $500M), and hundreds of millions in follow-on from Sequoia, Founders Fund, a16z, and General Catalyst. The overlooked truth: this is not a fund with a thesis. It’s a human-powered search engine for outlier founders, and the search algorithm is Cory Levy physically showing up at every campus, hackathon, and intern gathering in America, week after week, year after year.

Background

Cory Levy was born on October 10, 1991, and raised in Houston, Texas. While interning at Founders Fund, Justin Fishner-Wolfson advised him to leverage the relationships he was building, leading to the NextGen Conference at Stanford — an annual event connecting young entrepreneurs with established founders. He was organizing cross-network events for the Silicon Valley elite before he could legally drink.

Enrolled at UIUC for CS, met Michael Callahan, co-founded One, Inc. in 2011. Dropped out. Raised $1M at 19 over a tweet at Keith Rabois. Built After School (anonymous social network for high school students) to 20M users across 20,000+ schools. $16.4M Series A (Feb 2016). Team acqui-hired by Ancestry (2021). Left One Inc. in 2019, pivoted to what he does best: connecting ambitious young technical people with the right mentors and capital. Founded First Text, became Executive Director of Spearhead (AngelList/Accomplice), launched Z Fellows in 2020. Appeared on The Network State Podcast with Balaji Srinivasan (Dec 2025, Episode #23: “Where Talent Should Go”).

The Overlooked Angle

Everyone calls Levy “the Z Fellows guy.” That frames it as a program. It’s not a program. It’s a search operation. The core activity is Cory Levy physically showing up at 25+ university campuses per year, running fireside chats at midnight because builders work late, offering $10K hackathon bounties, reviewing thousands of text messages from founders, and personally conducting 10-minute interviews with a <1% acceptance rate. This is not how venture capital works. VCs wait for warm intros and pitch decks at Sand Hill Road. Levy is doing the inverse: going to where the founders are, before they know they’re founders.

The second dimension: the funnel is the product, not the check. $10K at a $1B cap is economically meaningless. What matters is the 500+ person alumni Slack, the 40+ mentor network (Netflix, DoorDash, Figma, Ramp, Anduril, Vercel, Reddit, YouTube), and the “very specific intros” that Levy and Baylor make — not mass emails, but personalized connections. This is why Hebbia’s founder texted Cory and ended up at Floodgate; why Nucleus went from “a one-man show in my bedroom” to $18M from Founders Fund.

The third dimension: the portfolio is self-reinforcing. Whop’s Steven Schwartz is now a Z Fellows mentor. Owner.com’s Adam Guild invests in other Z Fellows alumni (July). Nucleus’s Kian Sadeghi does the same. The alumni network is becoming its own deal flow and co-investment layer, which means the value of being in the Z Fellows network compounds — each successful exit makes the next cohort more attractive to both founders and follow-on investors.

500-800+ alumni embedded in top CS programs, AI labs, and startup communities. Natural scout-class talent for any early-stage fund.

6. Monitor Z Fellows cohort announcements as early warning

Levy’s tweets, Kevin Hartz’s endorsements, and cohort announcements on X are leading indicators. The pattern: Z Fellows cohort announcement → 6–18 months → pre-seed/seed round from top-tier VCs.

7. Build a direct relationship with Levy

His entire model is built on personal relationships. He responds to texts. He values people who show up. The highest-leverage play for any VC is not a formal partnership — it’s becoming someone Levy trusts enough to make warm intros to his best founders.
