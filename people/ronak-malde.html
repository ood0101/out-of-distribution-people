◆ Investment Thesis

Ronak Malde is one of a small number of people who has trained frontier software engineering models at both startup scale (SWE-1 at Windsurf) and big-lab scale (Gemini RL post-training at DeepMind). He left DeepMind after only ~7 months — walking away from an estimated $7.5–22.5M in unvested Google equity — and is now listed as “Stealth, prev DeepMind” on the Delta Institute Fellows page (Feb 2026). He has a specific, technically grounded thesis: continual learning (AI systems that self-improve from deployment experience). The Exceptional Capital Exceptional 100 — a list designed to identify future founders — already flagged him. The window to build a relationship is now, before he announces.

The Spike — With Evidence

At Windsurf (formerly Codeium), Malde trained the SWE-1 model family (SWE-1, SWE-1-lite, SWE-1-mini) from scratch — released May 15, 2025. No public SWE-bench score was published for the original SWE-1; Windsurf used internal benchmarks showing performance “close to all frontier foundation models” and “similar to Claude 3.5 Sonnet” while being more cost-efficient. The successor, SWE-1.5 (built by Cognition after acquiring Windsurf’s IP), scored 40.08% on SWE-Bench Pro across 731 tasks — comparable to Claude Sonnet 4.5 (43.60%) and GPT-5 High (41.78%) — at 950 tok/s (13.8x faster than Claude Sonnet 4.5).

SWE-1’s key differentiator was “flow awareness” — a shared timeline data model that captured contextual signals from terminals, browsers, and user feedback loops in the IDE. This is fundamentally different from standard fine-tuning approaches.

Google hired ~40 senior R&D staff from Windsurf’s ~150 total employees for $1.2B in compensation (~$30M per person on average). The ML model training team was likely single-digit headcount. Malde’s LinkedIn explicitly states he “trained SWE-1” — implying a direct, hands-on role, not a supporting function.

What He’d Build

The Continual Learning Thesis (from his own words)

His Jan 1, 2026 prediction: “2024 was the year of agents. 2025 was the year of RL. 2026 will be the year of continual learning.” Three technical bets underpin this:

  • Test-Time Training + RL (Stanford/NVIDIA) — He called the results “crazy”: models that adapt in real-time during inference. 35x faster than full attention at 2M context.
  • On-policy self-distillation — his “favorite paper of 2026”: the policy is both teacher and student. He sees this as bridging in-context learning with weight optimization.
  • Long-context RL — 5M context window on a single 8xH100 node. He noted: “Most people don’t realize that even on long context pretrained frontier models, most RL post-training is only done on a small fraction of that context.”

The through-line: AI coding agents that continuously self-improve from their own deployment experience, rather than requiring periodic retraining.
