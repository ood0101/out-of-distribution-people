◆ The Spike

In a single year, Liam McNamara went from “median Canadian pre-med” to serial founder with 7-figure ARR. The velocity is the signal. At 18, he turned his Queen’s University dorm into a biotech lab, built assays that won awards, co-founded Matrix Biotech (at-home hormone testing — all markers in 5 minutes for $5), built the first deep learning model predicting stroke recovery from fMRI in primates with a neurosurgeon at UCSF, ran a drug discovery project as the only undergraduate among 100 faculty in a Nobel Prize-winning lab, dropped out, cold emailed billionaires from a $4,000 Airbnb in one of the roughest neighborhoods in America, became a Z Fellow, then pivoted to Cybe (kernel-level data access governance for proactive breach prevention), got into YC W26, and hit 7-figure ARR in 2 months. He was teaching at MIT during his last year of high school. All of this before turning 20, from a Canadian town of 32,000 people with no Silicon Valley network.

The spike is not any single accomplishment — it’s the rate of change. The slope from pre-med dropout to YC-backed founder with real revenue in under 12 months, while simultaneously producing neuroscience research and biotech assays, is one of the steepest we’ve seen. The cross-domain range (neuroscience, biotech, cybersecurity, quant finance, deep learning) at this age suggests someone who learns entire fields, not just skills.

Background

Liam McNamara is from a town of approximately 32,000 people in Canada, likely in the Greater Toronto Area (featured on INSauga News, which covers the Mississauga/Peel Region). ~19 years old (described himself as 18 when raising his round in mid-2025, born ~2006–2007).

During his last year of high school, he was already teaching at MIT (specific role/program details from an INSauga News interview; exact capacity not publicly detailed). Entered Queen’s University in Kingston, Ontario on a pre-med / life sciences track. Turned his dorm room into a biotech lab with co-founder Krish Mendapara (his best friend from Queen’s), building quantitative blood alcohol assays that reached 85% accuracy in 2 weeks and won awards. This became Matrix Biotech.

Simultaneously conducted neuroscience research at UCSF, building what he describes as the first deep learning model to predict stroke recovery using fMRI in monkeys, working with a neurosurgeon. Also ran a drug discovery project as the only undergraduate in a Nobel Prize-winning lab with 100 faculty members. Work adjacent to a Nature Communications publication on AI-generated diagnostic insights from wearable devices.

Dropped out of Queen’s in 2025, quit a quant job, spent his last $4,000 on an Airbnb in San Francisco, cold emailed billionaires, became a Z Fellow (Cory Levy), raised a pre-seed for Matrix Biotech, then pivoted to Cybe (cybersecurity), got into Y Combinator W26, and hit 7-figure ARR in 2 months of market launch.

The Overlooked Angle

The obvious read is “teenage YC founder with traction.” That’s table stakes for the narrative. The actually interesting thing is the pivot signal. McNamara co-founded a biotech company, raised a pre-seed, and then abandoned it to start a cybersecurity company instead. Most 18-year-olds who raise money cling to their first idea. The willingness to pivot from something that had investors and momentum into a completely different domain (kernel-level DLP) — and then hit 7-figure ARR in 2 months — suggests an unusual ability to read markets rather than just execute on an initial thesis.

The research background is the other underappreciated signal. This isn’t a pure “hustle founder” who ships fast but lacks depth. He built DL models for neuroscience at UCSF, ran drug discovery in a Nobel lab, and turned a dorm room into a functioning assay lab. The technical range (neuroscience, biotech assays, kernel-level cybersecurity, quant finance) mirrors the pattern we see in the most effective technical founders: they don’t just learn tools, they learn fields.

The geographic signal also matters. From a 32K-person Canadian town, no family network in tech, pre-med at Queen’s (not Stanford or MIT), last $4K on an Airbnb — this is a cold-start trajectory. The Z Fellows selection (Cory Levy) and Pear VC backing (Arash Afrakhteh) are conviction bets on the person, not the pedigree.
