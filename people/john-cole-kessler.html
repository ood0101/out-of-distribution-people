The Spike

John Cole Kessler was fifteen years old when he co-founded Aaru in March 2024. Not fifteen and interning. Not fifteen and building side projects. Fifteen and co-founding a company that would raise a $50M+ Series A at a headline valuation approaching $1 billion less than two years later. The age is the least interesting thing about him. What matters is what he chose to build. Aaru creates synthetic audiences — AI simulations of human populations that replace traditional focus groups and surveys. Their Lumen model predicted the New York Democratic primary within 371 votes. The CTO role means he’s building the technical infrastructure for simulating human decision-making at scale — the hardest problem at the intersection of LLMs, social science, and behavioral economics. Before Aaru, he led the $4.5M seed round for Potato, a company building autonomous AI scientists for Stanford, MIT, and Caltech labs. His investment thesis — that biology is the next software — shows he thinks in platform shifts, not products. The Z Fellows selection confirms the pattern: he was identified as an outlier builder before Aaru existed.

Companies & Projects

Aaru (2024–)

Co-founded with Cameron Fink (CEO) and Ned Koh (President, Harvard dropout). As CTO, built the core multi-agent simulation (MAS) engine: generates ~5,000 AI agents, each endowed with hundreds of demographic attributes and behavioral cognitive patterns. Agents engage in 30–90 second simulated conversations, producing population-level predictions. Census data integration, proprietary datasets, and LLM-powered agent behavior.

Three product lines: Lumen (corporate simulation, integrated with Accenture Song), Dynamo (election prediction), Seraph (public sector/opinion dynamics). Predicted NY Democratic primary within 371 votes; backtested 2020 elections within 0.5%. The Overlooked Angle

The obvious story is “teenager co-founds billion-dollar company.” Every profile will lead with the age. But the structural insight is in what he chose to build and who he chose to build with. Aaru isn’t a consumer app or a coding tool — it’s an attempt to simulate human decision-making at population scale. That’s one of the hardest technical problems in applied AI, requiring deep understanding of LLMs, behavioral science, demographic modeling, and statistical validation. The 371-vote prediction accuracy on the NY primary is the proof point.

The Potato investment is the second tell. A teenager leading a $4.5M seed into autonomous AI scientists at elite research labs isn’t playing investor — he’s mapping the frontier of where AI meets physical-world science. The biotech thesis (“biology today feels like software twenty years ago”) is the unifying framework: he sees AI as the tool that makes other fields programmable. Aaru makes market research programmable. Potato makes laboratory science programmable. The pattern is consistent.

The CTO role at 15–16 is less about precocity and more about what it reveals about the team structure. Cameron Fink (CEO) handles the business and narrative. John Cole builds the systems. The division of labor suggests a company that’s serious about both the technology and the go-to-market, rather than a single founder wearing every hat.
