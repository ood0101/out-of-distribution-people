◆ LP Thesis — Why Sameer Gandhi

Sameer Gandhi is one of the most AI-exposed partners at Accel. He personally led or is responsible for Accel’s investments in Perplexity AI (board seat, led $500M round at $14B), Anthropic (Series G, 2025–26), CrowdStrike (board seat, ML-driven cybersecurity), Helsing (European defense AI), and Mind Robotics (industrial AI robots, board seat). He understands AI from first principles — his MIT EE/CS background and LinkedIn article on cybersecurity explicitly reference machine learning and behavioral analysis as investment theses.

The angle: Sameer’s portfolio companies (Perplexity, Anthropic, CrowdStrike, Helsing) all started as deeply technical teams at the frontier. An inception-stage AI fund that sources from frontier lab departures and competition winners is feeding Sameer’s exact pipeline — 2–3 years earlier than Accel can write checks. He also bridges Silicon Valley and India (led Flipkart from seed, flies to India regularly, backed Freshworks to Nasdaq IPO) — directly aligned with the Indian-origin sourcing thesis.

The ask: Personal LP commitment. Value prop is first-look deal flow on inception-stage AI companies that will be Series A/B candidates for Accel in 12–24 months. Accel already runs a “Starters” scout program ($200K per scout) — this signals institutional comfort with the emerging-manager model.

◆ What to Lead With
  • Perplexity connection: Aravind Srinivas is Indian-origin (IIT Madras). Sameer led the board seat. Open with: “You backed Aravind at Perplexity — we’re sourcing the next Aravind 2–3 years before they’re fundable at Accel’s stage.”

The Coefficient Bio acquisition by Anthropic (~$400M) is a concrete example: “We had Samuel Stanton on radar before Anthropic acquired Coefficient Bio.”
  • Indian-origin pipeline: He will immediately get this. Flipkart seed, Freshworks Nasdaq, Perplexity board — his career is built on backing Indian-origin technical founders. Frame the fund’s Indian-origin sourcing as systematic, not anecdotal
  • Frontier lab departure tracking: Show the 20-company departure watchlist. Sameer’s AI portfolio (Anthropic, Perplexity, Helsing, Mind Robotics) means he understands that the best companies start when exceptional people leave frontier labs
◆ How to Frame the Ask
  • Not co-investment (yet). The ask is LP commitment. But the value prop IS co-investment deal flow: “You get first look at the companies we find before they’re fundable at Accel’s check size.”
  • Accel already does this. Their Starters scout program ($200K per scout, 20+ scouts) shows Accel is comfortable backing individual operators/emerging managers for early-stage deal flow. An LP commitment in an inception-stage fund is the same model, professionalized
  • His signature move is early conviction + follow-on. CrowdStrike: every round. Flipkart: seed to Walmart. Marc Lore: three companies. Frame the fund as a new layer in his early-conviction pipeline
  • Check size context: Sameer likely has $50M+ in personal liquidity from Accel carry alone (Spotify, CrowdStrike, Dropbox, Flipkart exits). A $500K–$1M LP check is trivial for him. Don’t over-explain the economics
◆ What to Avoid
  • Don’t pitch the fund thesis abstractly. Sameer has heard 1,000 fund pitches. Lead with specific companies and specific people, not “we invest at inception in AI”
  • Don’t name-drop Accel back at him. He knows his own portfolio. Show you know it by referencing specifics (Aravind/Perplexity, George Kurtz/CrowdStrike) not by listing his companies
  • Don’t underestimate his technical depth. MIT EE/CS x3 degrees. He wrote about ML and graph data models in his cybersecurity thesis. He can go deep on technical architecture
  • Don’t position as competitive with Accel. This is upstream deal flow, not competition. Inception-stage ($500K–$1M checks) is 50x smaller than Accel’s typical first check
  • Don’t ignore the India angle. This is his career-defining thesis (Flipkart, Freshworks, Perplexity/Aravind). If you skip it, you’re leaving the strongest resonance point on the table
