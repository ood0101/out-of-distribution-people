◆ The Spike

Kunvar Thaman built a research career from scratch with no institutional runway. From BITS Pilani in Rajasthan, he cycled through neutrino physics (IceCube Collaboration), cybersecurity, NLP (GSoC 2019), and reinforcement learning before landing on mechanistic interpretability — the subfield trying to reverse-engineer what neural networks have actually learned. He created mechinterp.com as his research identity, wrote a 15-page open-problems reference document that became a community resource, co-authored “Benchmark Inflation” at ICML 2024 (showing LLM benchmark scores inflated by up to 16 percentage points due to training data contamination), organized hackathons with Apart Research, funded other early-career researchers on Manifund, and got hired at Standard Intelligence — all while operating as an independent researcher without a PhD, a FAANG pedigree, or a lab affiliation. Now he’s been admitted to MIT for a CS PhD.

The spike is the self-directedness. Most people in alignment research got there via elite PhD programs, MATS, or big-lab internships. Thaman built his own on-ramp: writing explainers, creating reference documents, organizing hackathons, securing Manifund grants for compute, and publishing at top venues — all from a standing start in India. The trajectory from BITS Pilani to Standard Intelligence to MIT PhD is one of the steepest slopes in the alignment community.

Background

Kunvar Thaman is from India. Online handle: firstuserhere (used across GitHub, X, Hugging Face, Coda, LessWrong). LessWrong display name: “Kayden.” ~25 years old.

Undergraduate at BITS Pilani (Birla Institute of Technology and Science), Pilani campus, Rajasthan (2017–2022). During undergrad, he participated in the IceCube Collaboration (the neutrino physics experiment at the South Pole), attending the spring meeting in Brussels in May 2022. Selected for Google Summer of Code 2019 with Red Hen Lab, building a Multilingual NLP Pipeline (mentors: Thomas Proisl, Vaibhav Gupta). Worked at Akamai Technologies and Netflix (engineering intern) before pivoting fully to ML safety research.

After BITS Pilani, he moved to San Francisco and joined Standard Intelligence (si.inc) as a Machine Learning Engineer — the aligned AGI lab founded by Galen Mead that builds computer action models, hertz-dev (8.5B parameter speech model), and operates a 30 PB data cluster. He built his independent research identity at mechinterp.com, published at ICML, organized hackathons with Apart Research, and has now been admitted to MIT for a CS PhD.

Benchmark Inflation: Revealing LLM Performance Gaps Using Retro-Holdouts
ICML 2024 · With Jacob Haimes, Cenny Wenner, Vassil Tashev, Clement Neo, Esben Kran, Jason Schreiber · Apart Research

Demonstrated that some LLMs have benchmark scores inflated by as much as 16 percentage points due to training data contamination. Introduced the “retro-holdout” methodology and released the Retro-Misconceptions dataset for TruthfulQA. Major implications for how the industry evaluates AI progress.

The Overlooked Angle

The standard path into alignment research runs through elite PhD programs, MATS fellowships, or big-lab internships. Kunvar Thaman took none of those routes. He built a parallel research infrastructure from scratch: his own domain (mechinterp.com), his own reference documents (Open Problems in Mech Interp), his own hackathon organizing (Apart Research), his own funding pipeline (Manifund grants for compute), and his own publication record (ICML 2024) — all without a PhD or institutional backing.

The breadth of his path is the other tell. Most alignment researchers have narrow, deep trajectories. Thaman went from neutrino physics (IceCube) to cybersecurity to NLP to reinforcement learning to mechanistic interpretability — each time self-teaching the domain, building projects, and moving on when the next frontier appeared more important. The hiring by Standard Intelligence (an OOD-level aligned AGI lab) and the MIT PhD admission validate the strategy: you don’t need the standard pipeline if you can build your own.

The infrastructure writing is also unusual. He blogs about data center cooling, power distribution, and GPU architecture alongside interpretability research. That hardware-plus-theory combination — understanding both what neural networks learn and how the machines that run them work — is rare in the alignment community.
