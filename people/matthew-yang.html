◆ Investment Thesis

Matthew Yang is one of the defining researchers of the test-time compute scaling paradigm — the idea that letting LLMs “think longer” at inference can be more effective than scaling model parameters. As an MS student at CMU, he co-authored three papers in 12 months that collectively establish the theoretical and practical foundations of this field: MRT formalizes test-time compute as meta-RL (ICML 2025), e3 shows in-context exploration enables extrapolation beyond training budgets (ICLR 2026, EXAIT Best Paper), and InT solves credit assignment for self-correction (ICLR 2026). His e3 recipe produced the best known <2B reasoning model on AIME ’25 and HMMT ’25, outperforming models 4–18x its size. He graduated Dec 2025 and has industry exposure at both Scale AI (LLM agent monitoring) and Skild AI (robotics foundation models, $14B valuation). With Aviral Kumar’s lab producing the most cited test-time compute work in the field, Yang sits at the exact center of the next paradigm shift in LLM inference. He is at the classic “PhD or startup?” decision window — the relationship should be built now regardless of which path he chooses.

The Spike — With Evidence

Yang’s core contribution is showing that in-context exploration is the key to making test-time compute extrapolate beyond training budgets. Three papers form a complete stack:

1. e3 (ICLR 2026 + EXAIT Best Paper at ICML 2025)

  • Co-equal first author with Amrith Setlur. Shows most existing reasoning models (including DeepSeek R1-distilled) do not extrapolate — performance plateaus or degrades beyond training token budgets
  • e3-1.7B: Qwen3-1.7B fine-tuned with their recipe extrapolates to 2x the training budget (32K tokens vs 16K training). Performance continues to improve as compute increases
  • Outperforms R1-distilled-1.5B and Agentica-1.5B on AIME ’25 and HMMT ’25. Outperforms some 7B and 32B models despite being 1.7B
  • Recipe: (i) chain asymmetric capabilities (generation vs verification), (ii) use “negative gradient” from incorrect responses to lengthen exploration, (iii) curriculum over data difficulty and token budgets

2. MRT — Meta Reinforcement Fine-Tuning (ICML 2025 + FM-Wild Oral at ICLR 2025)

  • Co-equal first author with Yuxiao Qu. Formalizes test-time compute as a meta-RL problem where each block in the output stream is an “episode”
  • Introduces cumulative regret as the measure of inference efficiency. Optimizes via dense “information gain” reward bonus
  • 2–3x relative performance gain and ~1.5x token efficiency over standard outcome-reward RL on AIME

3. InT — Self-Proposed Interventions (ICLR 2026)

  • First author. Solves credit assignment in LLM reasoning via self-proposed interventions — the model identifies where in its reasoning chain to intervene

Citation velocity: 147 citations (ACL 2023 compressor paper), 115 citations (MRT, 2025), 39 citations (e3, 2025). Total ~330+ citations across 8 papers. For a 24-year-old MS student, this is extreme.

What He’d Build

The Test-Time Compute Infrastructure Thesis

Yang’s research arc points to a clear product thesis: LLM reasoning should be treated as a meta-RL problem, where inference-time exploration and exploitation are optimized jointly. His work on MRT (dense progress rewards), e3 (in-context exploration that extrapolates), and InT (credit assignment for self-correction) form a complete stack for making any LLM reason better at inference time.

Combined with Scale AI (monitoring/evaluation) and Skild AI (robotics foundation models), the likely company would be in:

  • Test-time compute optimization layer — a reasoning engine that wraps any base model and makes it think more efficiently. The “search” between the model and the answer
  • Agent reasoning infrastructure — the meta-RL layer that enables LLM agents to explore, self-correct, and improve at deployment time
  • Efficient reasoning models — small (1–7B) models that outperform 70B+ on reasoning tasks via better test-time compute recipes

Market

  • LLM inference compute: projected $50B+ by 2028 as reasoning models (o1, o3, R1) consume 10–100x more tokens per query
  • Test-time compute optimization is the picks-and-shovels play: every company deploying reasoning models needs to make inference efficient
