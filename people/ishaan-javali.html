The Spike — With Evidence

NeurIPS 2025 Best Paper: “1000 Layer Networks for Self-Supervised RL”

Co-authored with Kevin Wang, Michał Bortkiewicz, Tomasz Trzciński, and Benjamin Eysenbach (advisor). The paper was one of only 4 Best Papers selected from 15,671 submissions (acceptance rate ~0.025%). Also selected as an Oral Presentation. Javali had a second paper accepted at NeurIPS 2025 as well.

The key finding: While most RL papers use shallow networks (2–5 layers), this work showed that scaling to 1024 layers in self-supervised contrastive RL produces 2x–50x performance improvements on locomotion and manipulation benchmarks — with no rewards or demonstrations. The trick: replacing value-based RL with self-supervised representation learning (InfoNCE/contrastive loss), combined with residual connections, LayerNorm, and Swish activations. “Their code doesn’t have a line saying ‘maximize rewards’ — it’s pure self-supervised representation learning.”

Critical depth phenomenon: Performance doesn’t gradually improve — it dramatically multiplies once crossing specific thresholds (e.g., 8 layers on Ant Big Maze, 64 on Humanoid U-Maze), triggering emergence of qualitatively different learned behaviors. Depth scaling also outperforms width scaling, achieving similar performance for 50x smaller models.

Why it matters: This is the first demonstration that the depth-scaling paradigm that drove progress in language (GPT) and vision (ViT) can also drive progress in RL. The paper proposes “deep teacher, shallow student distillation” — train frontier capabilities with 1000 layers, then distill to efficient inference models. This has direct commercial implications for robotics, autonomous systems, and any domain where RL is the training paradigm.

Five Building Blocks Identified

  1. Self-supervised learning + RL (Contrastive RL / CRL)
  2. Large-scale data from GPU-accelerated environments (JAX)
  3. Signal density via Hindsight Experience Replay
  4. Classification-based objectives (InfoNCE loss)
  5. Stabilizing architecture: residual connections, LayerNorm, Swish activation

Origin Story

The project originated from an Independent Work (IW) research seminar taught by Ben Eysenbach at Princeton. Kevin Wang and Ishaan Javali were undergraduates in Eysenbach’s lab. The work built on Eysenbach’s prior research casting contrastive representation learning as reinforcement learning. Infrastructure was key: JAX and GPU-accelerated environments allowed collecting hundreds of millions of transitions in hours.

RL-Native Infrastructure

The 1000-layer paper’s core insight — that self-supervised RL scales like language/vision when you use the right architecture — has direct commercial implications.
